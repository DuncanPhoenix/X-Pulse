Motivation




Modern 4th-generation X-ray facilities provide unprecedented opportunities for probing ultrafast dynamics. Their exceptionally high photon flux per pulse (109 - 1012 photons per pulse), combined with ultra-short pulse duration (~10 fs and ~100 ps at XFEL’s and synchrotrons) with pulse megahertz repetition rates, enables ultra-fast single-shot/single exposure imaging of stochastic phenomena in matter.

HISTORY



The history of speed photography evolved from capturing mechanical events visible to the human eye to using highly specialized lasers to probe subatomic-level ultrafast processes. Breakthroughs were driven by innovations in photographic technology and lighting, revealing hidden aspects of motion for both scientific analysis and artistic expression. Pioneers and chronophotography Eadweard Muybridge (1870s): Muybridge pioneered the photographic study of motion using a battery of cameras triggered by tripwires. His 1878 series, The Horse in Motion, famously proved that a horse lifts all four hooves off the ground at the same time during a gallop. This work was foundational for the development of cinematography. Étienne-Jules Marey (1880s): The French physiologist developed the field of chronophotography by using a single camera to record successive images of motion. He invented a "photographic gun" to take 12 pictures per second and later a continuous-film chronophotograph that was a precursor to the modern film camera. Marey's work often presented multiple exposures on a single plate to visually trace the path of movement. Georges Demeny and Ottomar Anschutz (1880s): Marey's assistant, Georges Demeny, refined chronophotography by creating the stroboscope for projecting images of motion. Independently, Ottomar Anschutz developed cameras with focal-plane shutters for exposures as fast as 1/1000th of a second and invented a viewer that used electric sparks to illuminate chronophotographs. The flash and high-speed strobe Early flash photography (1850s-1920s): The concept of flash photography dates to the 1850s, but early methods used dangerous, explosive flash powder. In the 1920s, flashes were still large, noisy, and smoky affairs. Harold "Doc" Edgerton (1930s): At the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), Edgerton pioneered the use of the electronic stroboscope to "stop" fast motion and produce crisp, instantaneous photos. His iconic images include a splashing milk drop forming a "coronet" and a bullet piercing an apple. Edgerton and his partners founded the company EG&G, which developed and produced high-speed photographic equipment. Strobe photography applications: Edgerton's work had both scientific and artistic applications. Scientific: High-speed strobe photography allowed physicists to analyze phenomena like fluid dynamics and air currents. Military: During WWII, Edgerton developed a powerful strobe system for aerial night reconnaissance. Artistic: His visually arresting images of motion won acclaim at art museums and inspired other photographers.


Ultrafast events and modern methods


Rapatronic camera (1950s): During the Cold War, Edgerton and his company developed the rapatronic (for "rapid electronic") camera to photograph nuclear tests. It used a magneto-optical shutter with no moving parts to capture exposures as short as 10 nanoseconds, allowing scientists to measure the expanding fireball from an atomic detonation.
Electronic imaging sensors (1980s-1990s): The invention and refinement of charge-coupled device (CCD) and complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) sensors revolutionized high-speed photography. Early CCD cameras had readout limitations, but engineers developed ways to increase frame rates. Later CMOS sensors offered high flexibility by allowing a tradeoff between frame rate and resolution, making high-speed photography more accessible.
Pump-probe spectroscopy (1980s): Ahmed Zewail pioneered the "pump-probe" method using ultrafast lasers to study chemical reactions at the femtosecond scale (\(10^{-15}\) seconds). The "pump" laser pulse provides energy to start a reaction, and the subsequent "probe" pulse photographs the molecular dynamics as they occur. This technique, known as femtochemistry, earned Zewail the Nobel Prize in 1999 and allows scientists to "watch" atoms and molecules as they move.
Compressed Ultrafast Photography (CUP) (2014): Researchers developed CUP as a single-shot imaging technique capable of capturing non-repetitive events at an astonishing \(10^{11}\) frames per second. It works by compressing a scene's temporal and spatial data and capturing it with a single exposure, later reconstructing the full video.
